Counting Strings - Part 2

Counting n length strings accepted by a regular language

Nov 27, 2020

I strongly recommend you to read Part-1 before proceeding into this section.

Now, let’s define the same for regular expressions. And then the fun begins

Given a Regular language \(R\), Minimal Regular Expression is the regular expression generated from the minimal DFA that accepts \(R\)

For example:
Let \(R_0 = (a+b+ab)^\ast, R_1 = (a+b)^\ast \).
\(R_0\) is not Minimal since we have two distinct ways of accepting the string \(ab\). But \(R_1\) is Minimal

Let \({\lbrack R \rbrack}_n \) denote the number of n length strings accepted by the minimal regular expression \(R\)

The following definations are valid for all Minimal Regular Expression

Defn. 1

\(R = \alpha \)

then, $$ {\lbrack R \rbrack}_n = \begin{cases} 1 &\text{if n = \(\vert \alpha \vert\) } \\ 0 &\text{otherwise} \end{cases} \tag{1} $$

Proof.: This is the base case. \(R\) accepts \(\alpha\). So, there is only one string of length \(n \) generated, i.e., \(\alpha\)

Defn. 2

\(R = R_1 + R_2 \)

then,

$$ {\lbrack R \rbrack}_n = {\lbrack R_1 \rbrack}_n + {\lbrack R_2 \rbrack}_n \tag{2} $$

Proof.: By defination \(R_1\) and \(R_2\) there will be no common prefix except \(\epsilon\), so \(R_1\) and \(R_2\) generate different strings

Defn. 3

\(R = R_1 \cdot R_2 \)

then,

$$ {\lbrack R \rbrack}_n = \sum_{\substack{0 \leq k \leq n}}{{\lbrack R_1 \rbrack}_k \cdot {\lbrack R_2 \rbrack}_{n-k}} \tag{3} $$

Proof.: Let \(s\) be one of the strings accepted by \(R\). Since \(R = R_1 \cdot R_2 \), we can generate \(s\lbrack :k \rbrack\) from \(R_1\) and \(s\lbrack k: \rbrack\) from \(R_2\), where \( 0 \leq k \leq n \).
Let \(k'\) be a value of \(k\) such that \(s\lbrack :k \rbrack\) is not accepted by \(R_1\) then \({\lbrack R_1 \rbrack}_k\) will be 0 \(\implies {\lbrack R_1 \rbrack}_{k'} \cdot {\lbrack R_2 \rbrack}_{n-{k'}} = 0\). In other words, \(k'\) does not contribute to \({\lbrack R \rbrack}_n\)

Defn. 4

\(R = {R}^\ast \)

then,

$$ {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_n = \begin{cases} 1 &\text{if n = 0 } \\ \sum_{\substack{1 \leq k \leq n}}{{\lbrack R \rbrack}_k \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-k}} &\text{n} \geq 1 \end{cases} \tag{4} $$

Proof.:
From the defination, \(R^\ast = \epsilon + R + R^2 + R^3 + \dots = \epsilon + R \cdot R^\ast \).
Applying (1) for \(n = 0\), and (3) for \(n \geq 1\) we get the result.

Example 1.

Find the recurrence for the number of strings accepted by \({\lparen a+bb+ccc \rparen}^\ast\)

Let \(R = a+bb+ccc \)
From (4),

$$ {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_n = {\lbrack R \rbrack}_1 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-1} + {\lbrack R \rbrack}_2 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-2} + {\lbrack R \rbrack}_3 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-3} + \dots + {\lbrack R \rbrack}_{n-1} \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{1} $$

For \(n \geq 4\), \({\lbrack R \rbrack}_n = 0\) since the maximum string length generated by \(R\) is \(3\).

So, $$ {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_n = {\lbrack R \rbrack}_1 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-1} + {\lbrack R \rbrack}_2 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-2} + {\lbrack R \rbrack}_3 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-3} \tag{eg.1.1} $$

Using (1), we have $${\lbrack R \rbrack}_1 = 1, {\lbrack R \rbrack}_2 = 1, {\lbrack R \rbrack}_3 = 1 $$

Substituing the values in (eg.1.1), we get,

$$ \begin{aligned} {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_n &= 1 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-1} + 1 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-2} + 1 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-3} \\ &= {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-1} + {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-2} + {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-3} \end{aligned} \tag{eg.1.2} $$

Base Cases:

$$ {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_0 = 1 $$ $$ {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_1 = 1 $$ $$ {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_2 = 1+1 = 2 \tag{\texttt{“aa”, “bb”}} $$

So, the solution is

$${\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_n = \begin{cases} {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-1} + {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-2} + {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-3} &\text{n} \geq 3 \\ 1 &\text{n = 0, 1} \\ 2 &\text{n = 2} \end{cases} \tag{eg.1.2}$$

Example 2.

Find the recurrence for the number of strings accepted by \({\lparen a+bb+cc+ddd \rparen}^\ast\)

Let \(R = a+bb+cc+ddd \)
From (4),

$$ {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_n = {\lbrack R \rbrack}_1 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-1} + {\lbrack R \rbrack}_2 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-2} + {\lbrack R \rbrack}_3 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-3} + \dots + {\lbrack R \rbrack}_{n-1} \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{1} $$

For \(n \geq 4\), \({\lbrack R \rbrack}_n = 0\) since the maximum string length generated by \(R\) is \(3\).

So, $$ {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_n = {\lbrack R \rbrack}_1 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-1} + {\lbrack R \rbrack}_2 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-2} + {\lbrack R \rbrack}_3 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-3} \tag{eg.2.1} $$

Using (1), we have $${\lbrack R \rbrack}_1 = 1, {\lbrack R \rbrack}_2 = 2, {\lbrack R \rbrack}_3 = 1 $$

Substituting the values in (eg.2.1), we get,

$$ \begin{aligned} {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_n &= 1 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-1} + 2 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-2} + 1 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-3} \\ &= {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-1} + 2 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-2} + {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-3} \end{aligned} \tag{eg.1.2} $$

Base Cases:

$$ {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_0 = 1 $$ $$ {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_1 = 1 $$ $$ {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_2 = 1+1+1 = 3 \tag{\texttt{“aa”, “bb”, “cc”}} $$

So, the solution is

$${\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_n = \begin{cases} {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-1} + 2 \cdot {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-2} + {\lbrack R^\ast \rbrack}_{n-3} &\text{n} \geq 3 \\ 1 &\text{n = 0, 1} \\ 3 &\text{n = 2} \end{cases} \tag{eg.1.2}$$

That’s all for part-2. Stay tuned for the next part!
